Step 0: The Building Blocks

Functional Groups in Organic Molecules

Before we get into the plants, we have to learn about the functional groups that give molecules their properties. A functional group is a specific arrangement of atoms within a molecule that determines the chemical behavior.

Aromatic Ring
Benzene C6H6
A stable six-carbon ring with delocalized electrons (shown as a circle inside). Found in many plant pigments.
Alcohol (Hydroxyl)
R-OH
An oxygen bonded to hydrogen. Makes molecules water-soluble. Found in sugars, menthol, linalool.
Ether
R-O-R'
An oxygen between two carbons. Found in many glycosides such as acemannan and linamarin.
Thiol
R-SH
Like an alcohol but with sulfur. Gives onions and garlic their strong smell. Less common in our garden.
Aldehyde
R-CHO
A carbon double bonded to oxygen at the end of a chain. Found in cinnamaldehyde and vanillin.
Ketone
R-CO-R'
A carbon double bonded to oxygen in the middle of a chain. Found in many plant metabolites.
Carboxylic Acid
R-COOH
Carbon double bonded to O, plus OH. Gives citric acid its sour taste. Found in ascorbic acid, which is Vitamin C.
Ester
R-COO-R'
Like a carboxylic acid but the H is replaced by another carbon chain. Gives fruits their sweet smells.
Amine
R-NH2
A nitrogen with hydrogens. Found in amino acids, chlorophyll, and nitrogen-containing plant compounds.
Amide
R-CONH2
A carbonyl bonded to nitrogen. The bond that links amino acids to form proteins in all living things.

Who lives in our garden?

French Lavender
Lavandula dentata
Ornamental Medicinal Pollinator friendly

The bush with small purple flowers on the leaves. The strong smell comes from volatile compounds released into the dry air.

Key Molecule
Linalool
C10H18O
Makes up 25 to 45 percent of lavender essential oil. Linalool is a monoterpeneTerpenes are compounds made from repeating 5-carbon isoprene chains. A monoterpene has two isoprene chains (10 carbons). They are responsible for most plant scents and are also present in essential oils. alcohol that interacts with GABA receptors in the brain, the same pathway some anti-anxiety medications use. It also works as a natural insect repellent.
Linalool Chemical Structure
Alcohol (OH)C=C double bonds
Functional Group Analysis: Linalool contains one hydroxyl group (OH), making it an alcohol. This OH group allows it to form hydrogen bonds with water, which is why it dissolves in water and easily evaporates in wet skin. The two carbon-carbon double bonds make it an alkene and contribute to its reactivity and ability to interact with receptor proteins in our nose and brain. The rest of the molecule is a hydrocarbon chain, which makes the overall molecule nonpolar, making it evaporate easily in the air.
Real-life uses in San Luis Potosi: Lavender essential oils (rich in linalool) are sold in local markets such as Mercado Republica for aromatherapy and as a natural repellent against mosquitoes. The alcohol group makes linalool volatile enough to fill a room with scent, while its ability to interact with GABA receptors in the brain explains why people use it to reduce anxiety. Many local artisans in San Luis also use lavender in handmade soaps.
Aloe Vera
Aloe vera (L.) Burm.f.
Medicinal Burn Relief CAM Plant

The spiky succulent with thick, fleshy leaves full of gel. It uses CAM photosynthesis (a special adaptation where the plant opens the tiny pores on the leaves only at NIGHT to save water during the day). This is essential for surviving the semi arid climate of San Luis Potosi.

Note about our garden: We classified two Aloe Veras but in fact they are the same but in different life stages, vegetative and reproductive. One is the main rosette plant (the classic Aloe vera), and the other is the same plant but in its blooming stage, sending up a tall yellow flower with pointy ends.

Key Molecule
Acemannan
Repeating unit: C12H20O10 (polysaccharide)
A long polysaccharide chain (a large molecule made of many sugars linked together) in the gel. It forms a protective, hydrating barrier on skin and has immune system moderating properties that help our body not to overreact. The gel is 99.5% water plus this molecule.
Acemannan Repeating Unit (Mannose sugar)
Alcohol (OH) - multipleEther (C-O-C)Ester (acetyl group)
Functional Group Analysis: Acemannan is full of hydroxyl (OH) groups on every sugar molecule. These OH groups form hydrogen bonds with water molecules, which makes the aloe gel so soluble in water and hydrating. Each sugar molecule is connected to the next through ether linkages (C-O-C bonds), called glycosidic bonds. The acetyl groups (esters, C=O bonded to O) attached to the sugars are what give acemannan its immune stimulative properties.
Real-life uses in San Luis Potosi: Aloe vera grows in many gardens across San Luis Potosi due to our semi arid climate. It is normally sliced open, and the gel is applied directly to sunburns and minor cuts. The hydroxyl groups in acemannan create a hydrating film on the skin, while the polysaccharide chains keep the skin moisturized. In the Huasteca Potosina, aloe vera is also mixed with water as a digestive.
Garden Geranium (Red)
Pelargonium x hortorum
Ornamental Perfume source Insect repellent

Those colorful red flowers that contrast the tire pot. The red color comes from anthocyanins, pigments that act as pH indicators. If you put a petal in acid, it turns pinker; in a base, it turns bluer.

Key Molecules
Geraniol + Anthocyanin
Geraniol: C10H18O | Anthocyanin: C15H11O+ (core)
Geraniol (the scent) gets used in expensive perfumes. Anthocyanins (the color) are strong antioxidants that change color based on pH, working like natural litmus paper.
Geraniol Structure
Alcohol (OH)C=C double bondsAromatic ring (anthocyanin)
Functional Group Analysis: Geraniol, like linalool, is a monoterpene alcohol with an OH group and two C=C double bonds. Its structure is very similar to linalool but arranged differently in a trans configuration (it is mirrored on different sides of the double bond), giving it a distinctly rose like smell. The anthocyanin pigment has a completely different structure: it contains aromatic rings (the stable six carbon rings with alternating double bonds) fused together, plus multiple OH groups. The aromatic rings absorb specific types of light, that makes the flower look red. The OH groups on the aromatic ring can gain or lose protons depending on pH, which is why the color shifts in acidic or basic solutions.
Real-life uses in San Luis Potosi: Geraniums are one of the most popular garden plants in San Luis Potosi. The geraniol they produce naturally repels mosquitoes. Rose geranium essential oil is used in local spas and in the creation of natural cosmetics.
Mulberry Tree
Morus sp. (likely M. alba or M. nigra)
Edible Fruit Silkworm Food Medicinal

The tree growing in a painted tire planter under the shade net. Mulberries have resveratrol, the same thing found in red wine that scientists study for possible longevity benefits.

Key Molecule
Resveratrol
C14H12O3
A polyphenol antioxidant (a molecule with multiple phenol groups, a phenol is an aromatic ring with an OH group) found in mulberry fruit and leaves. It activates SIRT1, a protein linked to cellular health.
Resveratrol Structure
Aromatic rings (x2)Alcohol/Phenol (OH x3)C=C double bond
Functional Group Analysis: Resveratrol has two aromatic rings connected by a carbon-carbon double bond bridge. It has three hydroxyl (OH) groups attached directly to the aromatic rings, making them phenols. These phenol groups are the key to resveratrol's antioxidant power: they can donate hydrogen atoms to neutralize free radicals (unstable molecules that damage cells). The aromatic rings provide stability through a phenomenon called resonance (the electrons are shared across the entire ring, making the molecule very stable even after donating a hydrogen) this also gives resveratrol the ability to absorb UV light, protecting the plant.
Real-life uses in San Luis Potosi: Mulberries are easy to grow in San Luis; the fruit can be eaten fresh or made into jams. Resveratrol supplements are sold across the country as antioxidants. In traditional uses, mulberry leaf tea has been consumed in many cultures. They are also a common food for silkworms.
Lime Tree (Limon)
Citrus aurantifolia
Food and Drink Vitamin C Aromatic

Growing in one of the big pots we can spot little limes that are a signature crop of Mexico. The scent of lime combines over 30 different volatile compounds. Essential in Mexican food.

Key Molecules
D-Limonene + Ascorbic Acid (Vitamin C)
Limonene: C10H16 | Vitamin C: C6H8O6
Limonene (the zest smell) makes up 50 to 90 percent of citrus peel oil. Ascorbic acid (Vitamin C) in the juice is essential for humans since we can't produce it.
D-Limonene Structure (Cyclic Monoterpene)
Ascorbic Acid (Vitamin C) Structure
C=C double bond (limonene)Alcohol (OH, Vit C)Ester/Lactone (Vit C)Carboxylic acid character
Functional Group Analysis: Limonene is an interesting compound because it is a pure hydrocarbon and it has NO oxygen-containing functional groups at all. It only has carbon and hydrogen, and one C=C double bond in the ring and one outside it. This makes it nonpolar, which is the reason why it evaporates so easily into the air as a scent and why it is an excellent solvent for grease. Ascorbic acid (Vitamin C) is the opposite: it has four hydroxyl (OH) groups and an ester (lactone ring, where the C=O is part of a closed ring with an oxygen). All those OH groups make it super soluble in water, which is why it dissolves perfectly in lime juice. The enediol group (two adjacent C=C-OH groups) is what gives Vitamin C its antioxidant properties, it can easily donate electrons to neutralize harmful free radicals.
Real-life uses in San Luis Potosi: Limes are fundamental to the food of San Luis Potosi and Mexico in general, the Vitamin C in limes historically prevented scurvy. Today, limonene extracted from citrus peels is used in household cleaners because its nonpolar nature that is able to dissolve grease.
Tomato
Solanum lycopersicum
Food Heart Health Antioxidant

Tomatoes are technically a fruit, but they are normally used as vegetables in cooking. Their red color comes from lycopene, one of the most powerful antioxidants in plants.

Key Molecule
Lycopene
C40H56 (a carotenoid)
Lycopene has 11 conjugated double bonds (alternating single and double bonds in a row), which is what makes it so effective at neutralizing free radicals. Cooking actually increases lycopene because heat breaks down cell walls and releases lycopene from inside cells.
Lycopene: A Long Conjugated Hydrocarbon
C=C double bonds (x11)Pure hydrocarbon (no O or N)
Functional Group Analysis: Lycopene is weird because it contains NO oxygen, nitrogen, or any other atoms apart from carbon and hydrogen. It is a pure hydrocarbon chain of 40 carbons with 11 conjugated double bonds. This allows the molecule to absorb blue and green light which gives tomatoes their distinct red color. The lack of any polar functional groups makes lycopene fat-soluble and not water-soluble, that is why cooking tomatoes with a little oil dramatically increases how much lycopene your body can absorb.
Real-life uses in San Luis Potosi: Tomatoes are very normal in gardens and in the agricultural regions of San Luis. In local dishes like salsa roja and salsa de chile de arbol, the tomatoes are roasted first therefore releasing more lycopene from the cells. Some studies suggest that diets that contain lycopene are linked with lower risk of certain conditions.
Parsley and Mint
Petroselinum crispum / Mentha sp.
Culinary Medicinal Volatile Oils

Mint produces menthol, the cold feeling you get doesn't actually lower the temperature. Menthol makes your brain's cold receptors (the protein TRPM8) active.

Key Molecule (Mint)
Menthol
C10H20O
A cyclic terpeneTerpenes are compounds built from 5 carbon isoprene units (5 carbons arranged in a Y configuration with an extra carbon on a side, containing two double bonds). They include monoterpenes (10 carbons, like menthol and linalool), sesquiterpenes (15 carbons), and diterpenes (20 carbons). Most plant scents come from terpenes. alcohol. It binds TRPM8 receptors and triggers a "cold" sensation without any actual temperature change. Used in cough drops and pain relief gels.
Menthol Structure (Cyclic Terpene Alcohol)
Alcohol (OH)Cyclohexane ring
Functional Group Analysis: Menthol has a cyclohexane ring (a six-carbon ring with all single bonds, so it isn't aromatic) with an alcohol (OH) group, a methyl group, and an isopropyl group (carbons in a Y configuration) attached to it. The OH group is the one behind its biological properties, it is positioned in a three dimensional arrangement that makes it fit perfectly into the TRPM8 cold receptor, the molecule is also moderately nonpolar thanks to the hydrocarbon ring and its branches, thus menthol evaporates easily and you can smell it.
Real-life uses in San Luis Potosi: Mint (hierbabuena) is an essential herb in cooking, used in prepared waters and as a garnish. Menthol is the active ingredient in products like VapoRub and cough drops, many apothecaries (yerberos) recommend mint tea for digestion, which has scientific sense because menthol relaxes smooth muscles in the digestive tract.
Cassava / Yuca
Manihot esculenta Crantz
Staple Food Cyanogenic Tropical Crop

The young tree with palm leaves, Cassava feeds a lot of people worldwide. But raw cassava is toxic because it contains a compound that can release hydrogen cyanide. the right cooking destroys it safely.

Key Molecule
Linamarin
C10H17NO6
A cyanogenic glycoside (Glycoside) means it has a sugar unit attached so that when plant cells are damaged, an enzyme called linamarase breaks linamarin apart, releasing HCN (hydrogen cyanide).
Linamarin Structure (Cyanogenic Glycoside)
Ether (glycosidic bond)Alcohol (OH, on sugar)Nitrile (C triple N)
Functional Group Analysis: Linamarin has two different parts, a glucose sugar and an aglycone (nonsugar part). The sugar has multiple hydroxyl (OH) groups making that portion water soluble. The sugar is connected to the rest of the molecule through a glycosidic bond (ether linkage (C-O-C)), The dangerous one is the nitrile group (carbon triple-bonded to nitrogen). When the enzyme linamarase breaks the ether bond connecting the sugar to the aglycone, the released fragment decomposes to release HCN (hydrogen cyanide).
Real-life uses in San Luis Potosi: Cassava is usually grown in the Huasteca Potosina region of SLP, the tuber is widely available in local markets. Yuca frita (fried cassava) is a popular side dish, and the proper cooking process ensures all cyanogenic compounds are destroyed. Tapioca, the starch extracted from cassava roots, is used in atole and other traditional drinks.
Torch Aloe / Candelabra Aloe
Aloe arborescens Mill.
Ornamental Medicinal Multiple flower spikes

The large blue or grey succulent with many branching orangy red flower spikes. This is a DIFFERENT species from Aloe vera. While they are both in the Aloe genus, Aloe arborescens grows in a branching, tree form, in comparison Aloe vera forms a single rosette.

Why two Torch Aloes in the garden? Similar to the Aloe vera situation, one is in a vegetative state (just leaves) and one in its flowering state (with orange spikes). They are the same species at different stages, or they may simply be two separate plants of the same species growing in different pots.

Key Molecules
Aloe-emodin + Aloin
Aloe-emodin: C15H10O5 | Aloin: C21H22O9
Aloe arborescens actually contains more aloin than regular Aloe vera. Aloe emodin is an anthraquinone (molecule built on three fused aromatic rings) with demonstrated antibacterial properties.
Aloe-emodin Structure (Anthraquinone)
Aromatic rings (x3, fused)Ketone (C=O, x2)Alcohol (OH, x3)
Functional Group Analysis: Aloe-emodin is built on an anthraquinone skeleton: three fused aromatic rings. The central ring has two ketone groups (C=O), making it a "quinone." This quinone structure is what gives the molecule its yellow-orange color (similar to how lycopene's conjugated system gives red color, the extended conjugation in anthraquinones absorbs light and produces color). The three hydroxyl (OH) groups on the outer rings and the CH2OH side chain provide some water solubility. The combination of aromatic rings and ketone groups allows aloe-emodin to intercalate (slide between) DNA base pairs, which is the mechanism behind its laboratory-demonstrated ability to interfere with rapidly dividing cells.
Real-life uses in San Luis Potosi: Aloe arborescens is commonly grown as an ornamental succulent in SLP gardens. In traditional medicine across Latin America, the gel and latex of this plant have been used for skin conditions. The higher aloin content compared to Aloe vera means it should be used with more caution. Some local herbalists prepare tinctures from the leaves, though it is important to distinguish the clear inner gel (safe) from the yellow latex layer (contains aloin, a strong laxative).

How Our Garden Captures Carbon

48% of every dry leaf in mass is pure carbon. That carbon was obtained from CO2 in the air through photosynthesis. Our garden is actively removing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere, contributing positively to the environment.

Two Strategies for Carbon

The dense leaves of our lavender bush have a low SLASLA stands for Specific Leaf Area. It is a measurement of how much surface area a leaf has relative to its dry weight. Thick, dense leaves have low SLA (small area per gram). Thin, broad leaves have high SLA (large area per gram). It tells us about the plant's strategy for capturing light versus storing resources. (they are thick and waxy), which means they store MORE carbon per unit of leaf area than thin leaves. Meanwhile, the mulberry tree has high SLA (thin, broad leaves), meaning each leaf is lightweight but catches more total light. Two different strategies, both effective at capturing carbon from the atmosphere.

Water Holding Capacity

Just 1% more organic matter means the soil can hold 20,000 extra litres of water per hectare. Our garden's dark soil near the rock wall holds much more water than the sandy desert soil found in the arid zones around San Luis Potosi.

Source: Soil Health Institute (2020)

CECCEC stands for Cation Exchange Capacity. It measures how well soil can hold onto positively charged nutrient ions (like calcium Ca2+, magnesium Mg2+, potassium K+). Higher CEC means the soil grips nutrients better and feeds plants for longer instead of letting nutrients wash away with rain.: How Soil Grips Nutrients

Humic acids (complex carbon-rich molecules in humus) have carboxylic acid groups (COOH) and hydroxyl groups (OH) that carry negative charges. These negative charges attract and hold onto positively charged nutrient ions like calcium (Ca2+), magnesium (Mg2+), and potassium (K+), preventing them from washing away in the rain. CEC is measured in meq/100g"meq/100g" stands for milliequivalents per 100 grams of soil. An equivalent is a unit that measures the charge-carrying capacity of ions. One milliequivalent = one thousandth of an equivalent. It tells us how many charged nutrient particles 100 grams of soil can hold. Higher numbers mean the soil holds more nutrients., which tells us how many nutrient particles the soil can hold per 100 grams.

Source: USDA NRCS Soil Education

The Living Underground

One teaspoon of healthy dark soil has more microorganisms than there are people on Earth. Bacteria, fungi, and worms all produce the humic acids that make soil dark and fertile. In San Luis Potosi, the calcareous (limestone-rich) soils benefit greatly from added organic matter to improve their fertility and water retention.

Source: USDA NRCS Soil Biology

Step 4: Shade and Temperature

Why is it cooler under trees?

It is not just about blocking sunlight. Trees cool the air through evapotranspiration: they release water vapor from their leaves, and the evaporation process absorbs heat energy from the surrounding air. Think of it as the same principle behind sweating. In a city like San Luis Potosi, where summer temperatures regularly exceed 35 degrees Celsius, this natural cooling is invaluable.

38 C
Bare Rock / Pavement

Rocks and concrete absorb heat because they have low albedo (albedo is how much light a surface reflects; dark surfaces absorb more and heat up). In San Luis Potosi, exposed pavement and stone walls can exceed 45 C on summer afternoons.

27 C
Under Plant Canopy

Evapotranspiration (which requires 2,260 kilojoules of energy per kilogram of water evaporated) plus shade makes the air under our lavender and mulberry noticeably cooler. That is real physics happening in our garden every day.

Daily Temperature: Bare Rock vs Plant Canopy in San Luis Potosi

Temperature data modeled from urban heat island studies. Source: US EPA Heat Islands program; Gill et al. (2007).

The Chemistry Behind Evapotranspiration

When water evaporates from a leaf surface, it requires 2,260 kilojoules of energy per kilogram of water. That energy comes from the surrounding air, cooling it down. Our aloe vera (using CAM photosynthesis) saves water by doing most of its gas exchange at night. Our mulberry and lavender transpire water throughout the day in the San Luis Potosi heat. A single mature tree can transpire 200 to 400 litres per day, removing as much heat as several residential air conditioners.

Source: US EPA, Trees and Heat Islands; USDA Forest Service, Tree Cooling Effects

Step 5: The Big Reaction

How does our garden eat CO2?

Every single plant in our garden runs the same master reaction, photosynthesis. Click each card to learn more about the stages.

6 CO2 + 6 H2O + sunlight energy -> C6H12O6 + 6 O2
carbon dioxide + water + sunlight -> glucose (sugar) + oxygen (the gas we breathe)

Light Reactions

H2O -> O2 + 2H+ + 2e-

In the chloroplasts (the green structures inside plant cells), chlorophyll (a molecule with a magnesium atom at its center) absorbs red and blue light. This energy splits water molecules, and the oxygen we breathe is a byproduct of this water-splitting reaction.

Our lavender's grey-green leaves contain chlorophyll wrapped in a waxy coating. The wax reflects ultraviolet light, protecting the chlorophyll from damage. The anthocyanins in our geranium petals also act as a natural sunscreen for the chloroplasts underneath the petal surface. Every leaf is a tiny solar panel that also filters its own incoming radiation.

Calvin Cycle

CO2 + RuBP -> 2x 3-PGA

In the second stage, the plant uses ATP (its energy currency molecule) to attach CO2 molecules together and build glucose. RuBisCO is the enzyme that catalyzes this reaction, and it is the most abundant protein on Earth.

Our aloe vera uses CAM (Crassulacean Acid Metabolism), a special adaptation where it stores CO2 as malic acid at night, then releases it for the Calvin Cycle during the day with its stomata (leaf pores) closed. This saves water in the dry San Luis Potosi climate. The mulberry and tomato use the standard C3 pathway, keeping their stomata open during the day.

Glucose Becomes Everything

C6H12O6 -> cellulose, lignin, terpenes...

Once a plant makes glucose, it transforms it into everything else: wood (lignin), fiber (cellulose), scent (terpenesTerpenes are a huge family of molecules made from 5-carbon building blocks called isoprene units. Plants produce them through the mevalonate pathway starting from glucose. They include scent molecules (linalool, geraniol, menthol, limonene), pigments (carotenoids like lycopene), and many medicines. Nearly all plant fragrances are terpenes.), medicine (flavonoids), and color (anthocyanins).

The linalool in our lavender is made through the mevalonate pathway, a metabolic route that converts glucose into terpenes. The geraniol in the geraniums uses the same pathway. The menthol in the mint is also a terpene. All of the amazing scents and medicines in our garden start with the same glucose molecule produced by photosynthesis. Carbon from the AIR becomes the medicine in the lavender oil.
